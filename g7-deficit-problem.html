The G7's Deficit Problem Isn't Going Away

From Washington to Tokyo, the governments that borrowed freely during the pandemic are now confronting bond markets demanding higher yields to finance still-expanding deficits. The era of cheap government borrowing may be over — with profound consequences for public services, investment, and growth.

Key Findings
  • The United States is running a fiscal deficit of over 6% of GDP in peacetime — a level historically associated with recessions or major crises, not periods of relatively strong growth.
  • Total G7 government debt now stands at roughly $50 trillion, with debt-to-GDP ratios in Japan, Italy, and the US all exceeding 100%.
  • Rising interest rates have dramatically increased the cost of servicing that debt: US federal interest payments are now running at over $1 trillion annually for the first time in history.
  • The political economy of consolidation — cutting spending or raising taxes — remains deeply unattractive in most democracies, suggesting deficits will persist at elevated levels.

There is a number that sits at the heart of the developed world's fiscal predicament, and it is not talked about nearly enough: $1 trillion. That is the annual amount the United States federal government is now spending simply to service the interest on its existing debt — more than it spends on defence, more than on Medicaid, more than on any single discretionary programme. And it is a figure that is growing, not shrinking, as maturing low-rate bonds are refinanced at the higher rates that have prevailed since 2022.

This is the fiscal reckoning that several economists warned about during the pandemic-era borrowing surge, and that bond markets periodically threaten to enforce. Understanding it requires stepping back from the immediate political debates — about tax cuts, spending increases, and budget standoffs — and examining the structural arithmetic of public finances across the G7.

How We Got Here

The debt levels weighing on rich-world governments today are the product of three compounding forces. The first was the 2008 financial crisis, which required massive bank bailouts and fiscal stimulus that pushed deficits sharply higher. The second was a decade of slow growth and low tax revenues that made those deficits persistent even as the acute crisis passed. The third, and most dramatic, was the Covid-19 pandemic, during which governments borrowed an additional $10–15 trillion globally to fund furlough schemes, business support, and healthcare spending.

What changed in 2022 was the interest rate environment. For a decade, governments had been able to borrow at near-zero rates — effectively being paid, in real terms, to take on debt. That made the debt-to-GDP ratios above manageable even at high absolute levels, because the annual cost of servicing them was trivial. Once the Federal Reserve, ECB, and Bank of England raised rates to combat inflation, the cost of refinancing that debt at maturity began to rise sharply. The US Treasury, which had locked in much of its borrowing at short maturities, has been particularly exposed.

"Governments borrowed as if interest rates would stay near zero forever. The bill for that assumption is now arriving."

The US as the Central Case

The United States presents the starkest version of this problem, partly because of its scale and partly because of the political dysfunction that has made addressing it so difficult. A deficit of over 6% of GDP in a year when unemployment is below 4% and growth is positive is, by the standards of macroeconomic theory, deeply anomalous. Deficits of that magnitude are normally associated with recessions, wars, or major crises — not with periods of relative prosperity.

The explanation is partly structural — mandatory spending on Social Security and Medicare grows automatically as the population ages — and partly political. Neither major party has shown any serious willingness to raise taxes or cut entitlements at the scale that would be required to materially reduce the deficit. The result is a structural primary deficit that compounds with rising interest payments to produce a debt trajectory that the IMF and the Congressional Budget Office both describe as unsustainable.

What Would Fiscal Consolidation Actually Require?

The scale of adjustment needed to stabilise debt-to-GDP ratios in the United States, France, Italy, and the United Kingdom is substantial but not impossible. IMF estimates suggest that a primary surplus (revenues exceeding non-interest spending) of around 1.5–2% of GDP, sustained over a decade, would be sufficient to put debt ratios on a declining path in most cases. That requires either spending cuts, tax increases, or some combination of higher growth that raises revenues organically.

The problem is that all three mechanisms face significant political and economic constraints. Spending cuts in entitlement programmes face fierce electoral resistance. Tax increases on working-age populations risk damping the growth that makes debt sustainable. And higher growth — the most painless path — cannot be conjured by policy alone. Japan's experience is sobering: decades of attempted consolidation have repeatedly been interrupted by downturns that forced the government to abandon its targets and borrow more.
