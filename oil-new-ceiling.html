Oil's New Ceiling: Why $90 Feels Distant Again

Demand from China remains soft, non-OPEC+ supply is surging, and the energy transition is — at last — starting to bite. For oil markets, the question is shifting from geopolitical risk premium to structural surplus.

By The Editors · 29 December 2025 · 9 min read · Commodities

Key Findings
  • Brent crude has traded in the $70–80 range for most of 2025, well below the $90–100 levels many forecasters projected at the start of the year.
  • Non-OPEC+ production — led by the United States, Brazil, Guyana, and Canada — has grown faster than demand, putting downward pressure on prices even as OPEC+ has maintained production cuts.
  • China's oil demand growth has slowed markedly as electric vehicle adoption accelerates: EVs now account for roughly 40% of new passenger car sales in China.
  • For the first time in the history of oil markets, analysts at several major banks have modelled peak oil demand scenarios within the current decade.

A year ago, the consensus view among oil market analysts was cautiously bullish: OPEC+ supply discipline would hold, Chinese demand would recover as the economy stabilised, and geopolitical risk from the Middle East would keep a floor under prices. Brent crude at $85–95 was the central case, with risks to the upside. The market had other ideas.

Brent spent most of 2025 trading between $70 and $80 per barrel — below the level at which several Gulf producers can balance their fiscal budgets, below the price assumed in many energy company investment models, and below the level that most energy transition advocates thought possible while global demand was still technically growing. Understanding why requires disentangling three separate forces, each of which would be significant on its own.

The Supply Surge

The first force is the extraordinary growth in non-OPEC+ production. The United States alone added roughly 1 million barrels per day of production between 2023 and 2025, driven by continued efficiency gains in the Permian Basin and other shale plays. Brazil's deepwater pre-salt fields have continued to ramp up. Guyana — a country that had essentially no oil industry a decade ago — is now producing over 600,000 barrels per day and is on track to double that by 2027. Canada's oil sands, despite their high cost and carbon intensity, continue to produce at record levels following the completion of the Trans Mountain pipeline expansion.

OPEC+ has attempted to offset this supply growth with production cuts, and the cartel's discipline has been better than in previous cycles. Saudi Arabia has borne a disproportionate share of the cuts, voluntarily reducing output to around 9 million barrels per day. But the cuts have not been enough to prevent the market from returning to a state of comfortable supply, and Saudi Arabia's patience — and its fiscal position, given its budget requires roughly $80–85 oil to balance — is being tested.

"OPEC+ is fighting a structural battle with a cyclical weapon. Production cuts can manage price fluctuations; they cannot reverse a fundamental shift in the supply-demand balance."

The China Demand Revision

The second force is the revision of Chinese oil demand expectations. For two decades, China was the engine of global oil demand growth — its industrialisation, motorisation, and infrastructure build-out absorbed an ever-increasing share of global supply. That story is not over, but it is materially changing.

Electric vehicle adoption in China has moved faster than almost any analyst predicted. EVs accounted for roughly 40% of new passenger car sales in 2024, up from around 6% in 2020. The fleet penetration of EVs is still low — most of China's 350 million vehicles are still petrol-powered — but the trend is clear, and it is beginning to show up in fuel demand data. Chinese gasoline demand appears to have peaked or be close to peaking. Diesel demand, linked to construction and freight, remains weak due to the ongoing property sector downturn.

The Energy Transition Signal

The third force is the most structurally significant: the energy transition is, for the first time, generating a discernible signal in global oil demand data. Global oil demand growth has slowed materially from its pre-pandemic trajectory, and for the first time, serious analysts at institutions including the IEA are modelling scenarios in which global oil demand peaks before 2030.

This does not mean oil demand is falling — it is not, at least not yet on a global basis. It means that demand growth is slowing, and that the long period during which oil demand reliably grew by 1–2 million barrels per day every year may be drawing to a close. For oil producers, investors, and the petrostates whose fiscal positions depend on high prices, this is a fundamental shift in the long-run outlook.
