Germany's Growth Problem Is Structural, Not Cyclical

The eurozone's largest economy has posted near-zero growth for three consecutive years. The explanations offered — energy prices, weak Chinese demand, post-pandemic hangover — are real but insufficient. The deeper problem is one of model failure.

Key Findings
  • Germany's economy contracted in both 2023 and 2024, marking its longest period of stagnation since reunification in the early 1990s.
  • Industrial output — the engine of the German model — has fallen by over 15% from its 2017 peak, with automotive and chemicals hit hardest.
  • The energy cost shock from Russia's invasion of Ukraine exposed a fundamental vulnerability: Germany's competitiveness was partly built on cheap Russian gas.
  • Structural issues — ageing infrastructure, chronic underinvestment, rigid labour markets, and a digitalisation deficit — predate the energy crisis and will outlast it.

For most of the post-war era, Germany was the economic envy of Europe. Its export-led model — world-class engineering firms, tight labour-management relations, a globally respected currency, and an innate cultural preference for fiscal discipline — produced decades of steady prosperity. It survived reunification, the dotcom bust, and the 2008 financial crisis with its reputation largely intact. The eurozone sovereign debt crisis made Germany look stronger by contrast.

That model is now in serious trouble. Not because of a bad quarter or an unlucky set of external shocks — though there have been plenty of those — but because the three pillars on which it rested have simultaneously weakened. Cheap Russian energy is gone. The Chinese export market is shrinking and becoming a source of direct competition rather than demand. And the internal combustion engine, around which Germany built one of the most sophisticated manufacturing clusters in the world, is being phased out.

The Energy Reckoning

Germany's industrial competitiveness was, for decades, quietly subsidised by cheap natural gas imported from Russia. Energy-intensive industries — chemicals, steel, glass, paper — could operate in Germany at costs that would have been unviable elsewhere in the rich world. When Russia invaded Ukraine in February 2022 and the subsequent sanctions regime effectively ended that supply relationship, German industry lost a structural advantage that had taken generations to build.

Gas prices in Europe spiked to roughly ten times their pre-war levels at peak. Even after the acute crisis passed, they settled at two to three times their historical average. For energy-intensive sectors, this is not a temporary headwind — it is a permanent repricing of their operating environment. BASF, the chemical giant and one of Germany's most iconic companies, announced plans to permanently downsize its German operations and shift production capacity to the United States and China, where energy costs remain lower.

"Germany did not simply suffer an external shock. It discovered that its competitive model had been resting on foundations that no longer exist."

The political response has been muddled. The coalition government that took power in 2021 broke apart in late 2024, partly over disagreements about how aggressively to use public investment to compensate for the structural shifts underway. The constitutional debt brake — Germany's self-imposed limit on government borrowing — has become a genuine economic constraint at precisely the moment when large-scale public investment might be warranted.

The China Problem

If energy was Germany's first structural vulnerability, China is its second. For two decades, German exporters — particularly in automotive, machinery, and luxury goods — rode China's extraordinary growth story. The German car industry in particular came to depend on China for roughly a third of its global sales volume.

That dependence is unwinding for two related reasons. First, Chinese domestic demand has been weaker than expected since the end of the zero-Covid policy, dampened by the ongoing property sector crisis and cautious consumer spending. Second, and more consequentially, Chinese manufacturers — particularly in electric vehicles — have moved from being customers to being competitors. BYD, SAIC, and Geely are now selling vehicles in European markets that directly compete with Volkswagen, BMW, and Mercedes.

The German automotive industry's transition to electric vehicles has been slower and more painful than that of its peers. Volkswagen — which employs roughly 120,000 people in Germany alone — announced plans in late 2024 to close domestic plants for the first time in the company's history, a decision that triggered the largest round of industrial disputes in Germany in a generation.

The Digitalisation Deficit

Germany's structural weaknesses are not limited to energy and automotive. The country ranks poorly on most measures of digital infrastructure and adoption relative to its GDP per capita. Broadband coverage in rural areas lags behind not just Scandinavia but many Eastern European economies. Public services are notoriously paper-heavy. Business formation rates in digital and tech sectors are low compared to the United Kingdom, France, or the Netherlands.

This is not a new observation. Germany's digitalisation deficit has been documented by the European Commission, the Bundesbank, and the OECD for the better part of a decade. The difference now is that it matters more. In a world where economic competitiveness increasingly depends on software, data, and platform businesses, Germany's strengths in precision engineering and industrial organisation are less decisive advantages than they once were.

Is There a Way Out?

The pessimistic reading is that Germany's model is broken and that the adjustments required — deep investment in digital infrastructure, a rapid energy transition, a restructuring of the automotive industry, and a relaxation of fiscal constraints — are politically difficult to the point of near-impossibility. German voters and politicians across the mainstream are deeply attached to balanced budgets and industrial employment. The institutions that made the old model work are resistant to the changes that a new model would require.

The more optimistic reading notes that Germany has successfully reinvented itself before. After reunification, a decade of stagnation in the late 1990s and early 2000s was followed by the Hartz labour market reforms, which — controversially — made the economy more flexible and laid the groundwork for the export-led growth of the 2010s. German engineering excellence and its dense ecosystem of Mittelstand companies — mid-sized, often family-owned specialists — remain genuine strengths that could be redeployed in new industries such as hydrogen, industrial automation, and defence technology.

What is clear is that the current trajectory is not sustainable. An economy of Germany's size and sophistication running at near-zero growth is not just a German problem — it is a eurozone problem, and increasingly a global one. Germany is the largest economy in Europe, the fourth-largest in the world, and the cornerstone of the European single market. Its prolonged weakness ripples outward through supply chains, trade flows, and political confidence in the European project itself.
