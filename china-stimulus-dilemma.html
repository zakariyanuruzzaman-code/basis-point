China's Stimulus Dilemma: Big Bang or Steady Drip?

Beijing has the fiscal firepower to mount a major economic stimulus. The question exercising policymakers, investors, and trading partners alike is whether it will deploy it — and whether it would even work if it did.

Key Findings
  • China narrowly met its 5% GDP growth target in 2024, but the composition of that growth — heavily reliant on exports and industrial output rather than domestic consumption — raises questions about its sustainability.
  • The property sector, which at its peak accounted for roughly 25% of GDP, has contracted for three consecutive years with no clear floor in sight.
  • Consumer price inflation has been near zero or negative for much of 2024–25, raising the spectre of a deflationary trap of the kind that has dogged Japan for decades.
  • Beijing has room for fiscal stimulus — its central government deficit is modest by international standards — but the political economy of deploying it is complicated.

The numbers China reports and the economy that those numbers describe have always been subjects of some debate. What is not in dispute is the broad shape of the challenge: a property sector in deep trouble, consumers unwilling to spend, young people struggling to find work, and a deflationary pressure building in the economy that conventional monetary tools have struggled to address. Against that backdrop, the question of whether and how Beijing will stimulate its way out has become one of the most consequential in global macroeconomics.

China's economy is the second largest in the world. What happens there reverberates through commodity markets, trade flows, and the earnings of multinational companies from Rio Tinto to Volkswagen to LVMH. For investors and economists, reading the direction of Chinese policy is not an academic exercise — it has direct implications for asset prices, corporate revenues, and emerging market stability across Asia, Africa, and Latin America.

The Property Implosion and What It Left Behind

To understand China's stimulus dilemma, you must understand the property crisis — and specifically what it has done to household balance sheets and confidence. Chinese households held an extraordinarily high proportion of their wealth in residential property: estimates suggest around 70% of household assets were in real estate at the market's peak. When prices began falling in 2021 following the liquidity crunch at Evergrande and a succession of other developers, that wealth effect worked sharply in reverse.

The scale of the contraction is striking. Housing starts have fallen by roughly 60% from their peak. The developers who pre-sold millions of apartments — taking deposits from buyers years before construction was complete — are now unable to deliver those units, leaving an enormous overhang of unfinished properties and burned household trust. Local governments, which had relied on land sales to property developers as their primary source of revenue, face severe fiscal pressures as that revenue has collapsed.

"China is not facing a property downturn — it is facing a property restructuring. The difference matters because restructurings take years, not quarters."

The government has taken a series of steps to stabilise the sector: cutting mortgage rates, relaxing purchase restrictions in major cities, and establishing funds to purchase unsold housing inventory. These measures have prevented a disorderly collapse but have not restored confidence. Buyers who watched neighbours lose deposits on unfinished apartments are in no hurry to re-enter a market that still looks overvalued relative to incomes in most Chinese cities.

The Deflation Risk

The deeper concern among economists is that China may be entering a deflationary dynamic — where falling prices cause consumers and businesses to delay purchases in anticipation of further falls, which depresses demand further, which causes prices to fall further. Japan's experience from the 1990s onwards is the cautionary reference point that Chinese policymakers are acutely aware of.

Consumer price inflation in China averaged just 0.2% in 2024, and has been negative in several months. Producer prices have been in deflation — negative territory — for over two years. These figures reflect genuine demand weakness: consumers saving rather than spending, businesses unwilling to invest, and a confidence deficit that monetary easing alone has proved insufficient to address.

Why the Stimulus Question Is Complicated

If the problem is clear, the solution might seem obvious: spend. The Chinese central government has relatively modest debt by international standards — its official deficit runs at around 3% of GDP, and total central government debt is well below 50% of GDP. Compared to Japan, the United States, or most European economies, China has substantial fiscal headroom.

The complication is political and structural rather than purely fiscal. First, Beijing has historically been reluctant to deploy the kind of direct consumer stimulus — cash transfers, consumption vouchers — that would most quickly address the demand shortfall. Its preference has been for infrastructure investment, which is more consistent with the CCP's industrial policy objectives but has diminishing returns as China's infrastructure stock is already extensive. Second, a large stimulus risks reigniting the property market before the underlying oversupply has been worked through, potentially creating a second, larger crisis down the line.

Third, and perhaps most importantly, the trust deficit between the state and consumers is not easily addressed by policy announcements. Chinese households are saving at historically high rates not because they lack money, but because they are uncertain about the future — about property values, about employment, about the direction of regulation in various sectors. Fiscal stimulus can put money in people's pockets; it cannot straightforwardly restore confidence.

The Global Stakes

What China decides matters enormously for the rest of the world. A major stimulus programme — particularly one oriented towards consumption — would provide a significant boost to commodity-exporting nations in Africa, Latin America, and Australia, lift demand for luxury goods and industrial machinery produced in Europe, and provide a meaningful tailwind to global GDP growth at a moment when the United States and Europe are both slowing.

Absent that stimulus, the global economy faces the prospect of its second-largest engine running at below potential for an extended period. The IMF's baseline forecast for Chinese growth in 2026 is around 4.5% — below the government's likely target, and below the rate needed to meaningfully absorb the economy's labour supply and address the youth unemployment problem. For a government that derives a significant portion of its political legitimacy from delivering prosperity, that is an uncomfortable position to be in.
